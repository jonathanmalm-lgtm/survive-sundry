The team huddle is at 9:00 AM. You are here because it is required and also because this is the last time today you'll be able to see everyone in the same room.

The worship leader acknowledges the staffing gaps — you're short this morning, which you already know because you've been managing it since 6:45. But — she pauses for effect — 'God won't give you more than you can handle.' She cites 1 Corinthians 10:13 with the confidence of someone who has found the perfect verse. The verse is about resisting temptation. It is not about staffing. Nobody says this. You think it. You continue to think it.

The campus pastor produces a $5 Chick-fil-A gift card. This was decided at staff meeting. The team nominated whose volunteer was most visibly on the edge. There was a consensus. He calls Maria's name. Maria has been working the welcome table since before you arrived this morning, has spoken with more first-time guests than anyone else on your team, and has smiled consistently for two hours in a way that is both genuine and quietly heroic. She receives the card with the expression of someone who didn't know they were being watched but is very glad they were.

Then Greg is introduced. Former worship pastor at a well-known church that recently imploded. He's here to observe. He is wearing a very good jacket. Greg scans the lobby with the eyes of someone who has stood in a lot of lobbies.

## A

Greg listens. Greg asks one follow-up question: 'How do you train for genuine warmth versus performative warmth?' You have a real answer. You give it. Greg writes something down. After service he comes back. 'That was the best lobby I've experienced in a while,' he says. You believe him.

## B

Safe. Greg moves on. Later you hear from the campus pastor that Greg mentioned the lobby felt 'polished but a little impersonal.' You think about that on the drive home.

## C

Greg tells you that his former church had a team of eight dedicated to first impressions with a separate budget line just for the lobby experience. He says this in the past tense, slowly, with the care of someone who is still figuring out how to talk about that season of their life. You listen. You ask one more question. Greg is different after that question.

## D:win

Greg greets people with the natural ease of someone who has done this for twenty years in front of large crowds. Three first-time guests stop to talk with him. He is warm, unhurried, and excellent. After service he finds you: 'I should have been doing this the whole time.' You don't entirely know what he means. You think you might.

## D:lose

Greg is a worship pastor. Greg's training is for the front of the room, not the door. Greg is warm but also slightly intense in a way that is appropriate for leading worship and less appropriate for greeting strangers at 9:40 AM. One first-time couple exits. Not leaves — exits. Through the parking lot. Dave clocks them. Dave updates his notes.
