The auditorium setup crew did their best. You know this. They were here at 6:30. They are good people. They also set chairs in rows of nine when the diagram clearly says eight, which means the side aisles are nine inches wide and the center aisle has a gap large enough to park a small car.

You have a laminated chair diagram. You have had this laminated chair diagram for three years. It lives in the facilities binder. The facilities binder lives on the shelf in your office. The setup crew has never, in three years, asked to borrow the facilities binder. You have not asked why. You are asking why now. Internally.

The worship team is already on stage running through their set. They are playing something in the key of G. The lead vocalist has her eyes closed. This is beautiful and inconvenient timing.

## A
You put down your clipboard and start moving chairs. You find your rhythm in about ninety seconds — pick up, step, set, step. It is meditative in the way that all physical church labor is meditative: slightly undignified, completely necessary, invisible to everyone who benefits from it. You finish in twenty-two minutes. The rows are correct. The aisles are passable. You laminate nothing new but you think about it.

## B
You decide that chairs are just chairs and proximity to God is not determined by row width. This is theologically defensible. Practically, the side aisles cause a traffic jam during every worship transition, a woman trips over the end of a chair in row four, and the camera operator — already unhappy with sightlines — moves his tripod six inches left and knocks over a plant. The plant was fake. This does not make it better.

## C
You catch the bassist's eye between songs and mime "can you help" while pointing at the chairs. He looks at the chairs, looks at you, and nods in that way that bassists nod — slow, unbothered, completely reliable. He recruits two other musicians. They move chairs with the loose efficiency of people who set up and tear down stages twice a week. The room is reset in eleven minutes. The lead vocalist never opens her eyes. This is fine.

## D:win
You walk to the stage, wait for a break in rehearsal, and explain to the worship leader that you're experimenting with "wide-aisle seating for movement and response." She lights up. She uses the phrase "freedom to respond" three times in the next twenty minutes. The congregation responds more during worship than usual — partly because the concept landed, partly because the extra aisle space genuinely makes them less afraid to move. Nobody knows this was a chair diagram problem.

## D:lose
You tell two volunteers the spacing is "intentional." They tell the greeters. The greeters tell guests. By second service, a first-time attendee asks the greeter why the church doesn't have enough chairs. The greeter confidently says it's "intentional spacing for movement." The guest nods thoughtfully. "So you know it's not enough chairs." You are told about this conversation at the H&M meeting.
