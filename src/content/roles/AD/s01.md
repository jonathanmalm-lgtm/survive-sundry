You arrive at 6:58 AM because 7:00 AM is late in church world and you've known this since 2011. The lobby smells like carpet cleaner and something else — something warm, mechanical, and wrong. You locate the source immediately: the HVAC unit above the east wing is making a sound that HVAC units are not supposed to make. It is a wet, rhythmic clicking. You have heard this sound before. It cost $4,200 before.

The thermostat reads 62°F. It is March. The unit is running but producing nothing. You check the facilities log on your clipboard. The last maintenance note says "looked fine" and is dated fourteen months ago. The person who wrote that note no longer works here. You are the person who works here.

First service is in three hours. Two hundred people will sit in this room. The senior pastor will not know about this until it is already fixed or already a disaster. That is the job.

## A
You call Terry — not the HVAC company on the approved vendor list, but Terry specifically, because Terry answers on Sunday mornings and owes you one from the incident with the burst pipe in December. He picks up on the second ring. He says he can be there by 8:30. He also says "this is the last time" in a tone that suggests it is not the last time. The unit is running by 9:15. The lobby reaches 68°F before first service. Nobody knows. That's the job.

## B
You write "HVAC — east unit — clicking noise — needs attention ASAP" on a sticky note in your best handwriting and affix it to the thermostat. You return to your clipboard. By 9:20, a volunteer named Brenda has removed the sticky note because it looked messy. The sanctuary reaches 59°F during worship. Three people leave. One of them tweets about it. The tweet says "ice church 🥶" and gets fourteen likes. You find out at the Hits & Misses meeting.

## C
Dave sees you walking toward the parking lot at 7:03 AM and gives you the thumbs up before you've said a word. You explain the clicking. He tilts his head. He says "east unit?" You say yes. He says "that's a capacitor." He pulls out his phone and calls someone named Hector. Hector shows up at 8:10 with a white van that has no logo. The unit works fine. You never learn Hector's last name and you will not ask.

## D:win
You spend forty-five minutes reorganizing signage, moving the check-in stations, and telling every volunteer that the fellowship hall "has always had better acoustics anyway." It's a lie but you say it with such administrative confidence that three volunteers repeat it to newcomers like it's a selling point. The east wing stays closed. Everyone assumes it's intentional. The pastor compliments "the flow" in the H&M meeting. You do not correct him.

## D:lose
The pivot makes sense on paper. In execution, the fellowship hall check-in creates a traffic bottleneck at the coffee station, two families end up in the wrong room, and a first-time guest spends eleven minutes in the hallway before anyone helps her. She fills out a connection card anyway. Under "How did you hear about us?" she writes "Google. Also the hallway was confusing." You find the card Monday morning. You keep it.
