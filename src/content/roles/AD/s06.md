First service is running. You have seventeen minutes of relative quiet.

The volunteer database has a problem. Planning Center sent you a report this morning and you've been sitting on it because the morning was too full to address it. Now you're addressing it. The problem: a sync error has pushed 34 volunteer records to 'inactive.' These volunteers are actively serving today. They are physically present. They are not, according to the database, supposed to be here. Technically, per protocol, they should have received background check renewal notices last month. Per the database, they didn't receive anything because they were marked inactive.

If anyone asks, you cannot confirm these 34 people are cleared. You can visually confirm they're here and they're the same people who have been serving for years. That is not the same as the system confirming it.

## A

You work through seventeen records before service ends. The methodology is tedious and correct. You finish the remaining records during turnover. By second service the database is clean. Nobody ever knows it wasn't. This is the job.

## B

You send a brief, direct message to the 34 volunteers explaining there was a sync error, they are confirmed active, and you'll have it resolved in the system by Monday. Twelve of them reply. Eight of them say 'thank you.' Four of them ask questions you didn't anticipate. You answer each question individually. By the time first service ends you have managed a database error and four small pastoral moments.

## C

Monday morning. Campus pastor asks why three of the 34 volunteers received background check renewal notices over the weekend — the system apparently self-corrected partway through and sent automated alerts to a subset of records before stopping. Three volunteers think their clearance expired. Two of them call the church. One of them calls from the parking lot of the church, during second service, in real time. You find out about this on Monday. The campus pastor finds out about this Monday afternoon. Monday is long.

## D:win

The bulk delete works. The rebuild from your master spreadsheet takes 38 minutes. The database is clean by turnover. Your master spreadsheet, which you maintain in addition to the official database because you don't entirely trust the official database, is once again vindicated. You will not mention the master spreadsheet exists. Some things do not need to be explained.

## D:lose

The bulk delete removes 34 records and also, due to a relational dependency you didn't account for, removes 11 additional records that were linked to the 34. You have now removed 45 volunteer records. The rebuild is possible. The rebuild will take until Tuesday. The campus pastor discovers the database state Monday morning when he tries to pull a report. His text says: 'What happened to the volunteer records.' It is not a question. It is not a question in a way that feels significant.
