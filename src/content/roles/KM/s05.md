It's 9:50. Service starts at ten. Families are already walking in — the early ones, the ones who've done this before, who know the parking situation and leave accordingly. You can hear the lobby getting louder through the double doors.

Your rooms are staffed. The supplies are out. The check-in kiosks are on. Noah has already done two different animal voices and it's not even service time. Diane is in the infant room looking like the person you want running the infant room. Jackson is in the elementary room wearing his name tag (whatever name it says — you're calling it a win).

You have done the work. You are ready. You have four minutes before the doors open.

This is the part nobody tells you about: the moment right before it starts when everything is either completely fine or catastrophically about to not be, and you can't tell which yet.

You take one breath. It smells like lavender spray and dry-erase markers and coffee you haven't finished.

## A
You walk every room in three minutes flat. Infant: Diane has it. Two-year-old: Tyler is ready, looks slightly terrified in the best way. Preschool: Noah is mid-animal-voice. Elementary: six kids are already seated and one is Jackson, who raises a hand when he sees you. You don't know what he wants but you give him a thumbs-up. He seems satisfied. You are back at the main door by 9:54 with a full picture of what you're walking into. That feeling is worth everything.

## B
You stand at the welcome desk just inside the kids wing entrance and arrange your face into the expression of someone who has never once been caught off guard. Families walk in and you greet them warmly and with great confidence. Everything is fine until the two-year-old room volunteer radios at 9:58 to say the door handle is stuck and she can't get the door open from the inside. You did not do the walk-through. You did not catch this. The next four minutes are instructive.

## C
You knock on the infant room door frame. Diane looks up. "I'm fine," she says, before you say anything. "We have four babies registered and I have four diapers, four bottles labeled, and two backup outfits per child." She holds up a small laminated card with every registered infant's name and a note about each one. You didn't ask her to make that. She made it anyway. You tell her she's incredible. She says "I know" and turns back to rearranging the burp cloths.

## D:win
You find the small alcove between the elementary room and the storage closet, lean against the wall, and close your eyes for ninety seconds. Nobody sees you. You think about exactly one thing: you are about to spend the next three hours helping kids have the safest, most joyful experience of their week. You open your eyes. You are ready in a way that goes past logistics. The first family through the door gets your best welcome of the day.

## D:lose
You find the alcove. You close your eyes. You are more tired than you realized. You are in that alcove for four minutes and twenty seconds, which is twenty seconds after service started, which is the moment a family with a first-time guest arrives at an unmanned welcome desk and stands there reading the laminated FAQ sign for longer than any first-time guest should ever have to stand anywhere reading anything.
