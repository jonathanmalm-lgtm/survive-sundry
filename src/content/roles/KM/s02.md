You have twelve volunteer slots. Seven people are present. Of the five missing: two texted last night (sick, sorry, love you), one texted this morning at 7:18 (car trouble, probably), one hasn't texted at all, and one — Brenda — is almost certainly here but cannot be found. Brenda does this. Brenda always turns up eventually, usually in a room nobody assigned her to, singing softly and arranging chairs.

Your elementary room is fine. Your preschool room is fine. Your infant room has Diane, who could run the infant room alone during a Category 4 hurricane and probably has. The problem is the two-year-old room, which currently has one volunteer and the residual smell from earlier, and which will have fourteen children in approximately two and a half hours.

You need one more warm body in that room. Preferably someone who does not cry around toddlers.

## A
You open the sub list on your phone. It's laminated in your binder too, but your phone is faster. First call: voicemail. Second call: voicemail. Third call: Tyler, 22, answers on the first ring, says "yeah totally" before you finish the sentence, and arrives at 8:45 in a hoodie with a reasonable attitude. You don't know much about Tyler. But Tyler is here, and Tyler is willing, and today that is sufficient.

## B
You quietly shift Noah from preschool to the two-year-old room and tell the preschool team to "flex together." You don't explain the math. The preschool team is now one person short. They figure it out. Nobody says anything. But Kayla, your preschool lead, gives you a look during the huddle that you will need to address in a one-on-one this week.

## C
You text Brenda first: "Hey, just checking in — all good?" Then you text the no-show: "Hey, thinking of you — everything okay?" Brenda replies: "I'm in the nursing mothers lounge, I thought I was on quiet room this week?" (She was not.) The no-show doesn't reply until 2:30 PM: "Oh no, I completely forgot. I'm so sorry." You handle the two-year-old gap with a reshuffle. It works, barely. But Brenda feels seen, and that matters.

## D:win
You walk directly to the pre-stage holding area where the worship team is warming up and make eye contact with a guy holding a coffee cup who looks like he might say yes. His name is Brett. He was a camp counselor for six years. He is delightful in the two-year-old room and three parents ask if he's on staff. He is not. You have his number now.

## D:lose
You walk into the pre-stage area mid-worship-team-prayer. You wait. It takes longer than expected. The worship pastor opens one eye and looks at you. You explain the situation in a whisper. The person who volunteers is a guitarist who has never been in a kids room before and does not understand ratios. He spends forty minutes trying to lead a toddler in "Good Good Father." The two-year-olds are fine. He is rattled.
