Turnover between services. You're resetting the rooms, briefing the second-service volunteers, and attempting to eat half a granola bar standing up.

Your youngest volunteer, who is sixteen and very earnest, comes to find you. There's a snack issue. The snack for second service is trail mix. The trail mix contains peanuts. A child checking in for second service has a severe peanut allergy. The child's allergy form is in the system. The volunteer who ordered the snacks was not the volunteer who checks the allergy forms. Nobody did anything wrong. This is just how it happened.

The child is fine. The child has not eaten anything yet. You have about four minutes before the parents leave the building.

## A

You pull the trail mix from every room. You find an emergency backup snack — goldfish crackers, which have been in your cabinet since October and are technically still valid. Second service kids eat slightly stale goldfish and are completely unaffected by this. You update the snack protocol Monday morning. Nobody ever knows this happened.

## B

You find them in the second row. The father is already standing up. The mother looks at you with the expression of a parent who has prepared for exactly this moment. They are calm. They are grateful. They handle it. The child gets cheerios from the diaper bag and considers this a win. You update the protocol Monday.

## C

The allergy list says 'tree nuts, peanuts.' The trail mix absolutely contains peanuts. You look at the list for three seconds hoping the list will change. The list does not change.

## D:win

You find the EpiPen. You review the instructions. The child does not need the EpiPen because you pulled the snack in time. But you had the EpiPen ready and you were prepared and the campus pastor, when he hears about this later, says: 'You handled that exactly right.' You accept this. You update the protocol Monday.

## D:lose

The child does not have a reaction because you caught it in time. But the campus pastor hears you described the situation to a parent as 'I had the EpiPen ready to go.' The parent asks: 'Was there a reaction?' You say: 'No, it was precautionary.' The campus pastor pulls you aside after the service. He is not angry. He is precise. 'We don't position ourselves as the first line of medical response,' he says. 'We call 911 and the parents.' You know this. You knew this. You file it.
