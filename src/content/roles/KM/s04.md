The all-staff huddle is mandatory and you know better than to skip it. You are standing in the main hallway at 9:00 AM, coffee in hand, surrounded by every ministry leader and volunteer the church could locate on a Sunday morning. The campus pastor opens with a story about his kids that is longer than it needs to be but genuinely sweet. Someone gives a speech about how "we get to do this." You nod.

Then a verse is cited — 1 Corinthians 10:13, offered as encouragement about being short-staffed. It is not about that. You know it is not about that. You make a note to maybe, gently, some other time, say something. Not today.

The campus pastor gives a $5 Chick-fil-A gift card to Noah because Noah is visibly running on nothing and has done animal voices for forty minutes straight without being asked. Noah accepts it with the energy of a man who has made peace with his situation. Then Greg is introduced. Greg is new to the volunteer rotation, came highly recommended through what someone calls "the Dante network." His former church — he mentions it in present tense at first, then corrects to past tense — was apparently quite something. The jacket confirms it. It is an exceptional jacket. You wonder briefly what the monthly lighting budget was. It was more than Greg's salary. You can tell.

Then your radio crackles. It's the kids wing. Jackson is here. Jackson is seven. Jackson identifies as a dragon. Jackson prayed for a Nintendo Switch last week and is now waiting on God's timing. Jackson will not wear his name tag because, he explains, dragons don't have names — they have titles.

## A
You walk back to the wing and crouch down to Jackson's level. "Okay," you say. "What's your title?" He thinks about it very seriously. "Flame-Claw the Destroyer." You write it on the name tag in your neatest printing. He puts it on without complaint. His mom, watching from the door, mouths "thank you" in a way that means more than she's saying. Jackson wears the name tag all morning. Flame-Claw the Destroyer is a model participant.

## B
You radio back: "Just let him be in the room, we'll figure out check-in at pickup." The check-in system flags it as an unregistered child at 10:47. His mom arrives at pickup and the system asks for a matching tag. There is no tag. She is understanding. The system is not. You spend twelve minutes at the kiosk explaining the situation to a volunteer who just started last week.

## C
You slip out of the tail end of the huddle and find Jackson near the elementary room door. You sit down on the floor — full commitment — and ask him about dragons. He tells you that his specific dragon can breathe fire but chooses not to because fire is a responsibility. You talk about responsibility for a while. By the end, he puts on the name tag himself. It takes nine minutes. It was worth nine minutes.

## D:win
You get on his level and say, very seriously, "You know what's interesting about dragons? The brave ones always let the villagers know who they are. So the villagers don't get scared." Jackson considers this for four full seconds. Then he holds out his hand for the name tag. You write his name. He wears it. He also tells every adult in the room he is a dragon. They all take it very well.

## D:lose
You tell Jackson that dragons are very brave about name tags. Jackson informs you that actually dragons guard treasure and do not take instructions from villagers. He has thought about this more than you have. He will not wear the name tag. He also tells Noah. Noah sides with the dragon, instinctively and without hesitation. You respect it and also wish it hadn't happened.
