The setup team arrived at 8:00 and worked efficiently and with great enthusiasm. This is, it turns out, the problem. Jordan — nineteen, eager, blessed — followed last week's supply list instead of this week's and has cut forty-seven paper shields out of white cardstock when today's lesson on the Armor of God calls for paper plates. There are no paper plates. There are also no more pieces of cardstock, because Jordan used them all. The shields are actually beautiful. They are the wrong shape for the craft entirely.

Today's guest, if she shows up, has a seven-year-old daughter. The whole lesson is the paper plate shield. The paper plate shield is on the take-home card. The paper plate shield is in the parent email that went out Thursday.

You have ninety minutes to solve this.

## A
You look at the forty-seven beautiful white shields and make a decision: paper shields are better than paper plates. You grab a marker, adjust the craft instructions by hand, and retitle it "The Shield of Faith — Custom Edition." You give Noah a three-sentence briefing. He improvises an object lesson about how David didn't use standard-issue armor either. The kids are into it. One of them names his shield. You will update the curriculum notes on Monday.

## B
You fire off a text to Leslie, the curriculum coordinator: "Hey — Jordan used the wrong supply list and we have no paper plates. What do we do?" Leslie is currently at brunch with her family. She replies at 9:40 with "Oh no! Maybe grab some from the kitchen?" There are no paper plates in the kitchen. There are paper towels and one sleeve of Styrofoam cups. You do your best with the cups. It is not your best.

## C
You gather your four present volunteers and explain the situation in thirty seconds. The room gets quiet. Then Diane says, "We have paper bowls in the infant room." You all look at each other. A paper bowl is not a shield. But a paper bowl is round, and round is closer to a plate than a rectangle. Noah says he can make it work. He does. The kids are mildly confused and completely fine.

## D:win
You throw out the instructions entirely. You hand every kid a cardstock shield and a marker and say "decorate your armor." It becomes the best craft in recent memory. Six kids draw their pets. Jackson draws a dragon. One child writes the word "STRONG" in very large letters and you quietly photograph it for the Instagram. The parents love the shields. Three take photos. Jordan feels vindicated.

## D:lose
You decide structure is overrated and let the kids freestyle with the cardstock shields and every available marker. The room has twenty-three elementary-age children with uncapped Sharpies and what they interpret as permission. By 9:15, two tables have been signed. A child has written their full name and address on the wall near the door, apparently from memory. The craft is a hit. The cleanup is not.
