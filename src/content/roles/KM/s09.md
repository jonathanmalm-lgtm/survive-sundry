Second service is running. A volunteer appears at your office door. She is nineteen, she is new, and she has a look on her face that you have learned to read. It is the look of someone who has been managing a situation for longer than the situation should have been managed.

The toddler room has a child who has been crying for eighteen minutes. Not the I'm-adjusting cry. The sustained, committed, theological-level cry of a child who knows her mother is somewhere in this building and has decided that this is unacceptable. Two volunteers have tried. The other toddlers are beginning to absorb the energy. It has the potential to cascade.

## A

You walk in. The crying child looks at you. She cries louder. This is normal. You pick her up. You do the sway. You've done the sway ten thousand times. The sway works. It always works eventually. She winds down over four minutes while you continue the sway. The room stabilizes. You hand her back with the quiet confidence of a woman who has been doing this for nine years.

## B

The parents come out. The mom takes one look and says 'oh baby.' The dad looks guilty. The child stops crying within forty-five seconds of contact. They sit in the hallway until she's ready to go back in. She goes back in. She's fine. The parents return to service and are probably slightly less present for the rest of the sermon.

## C

The second volunteer sees the text eight minutes later. She's in the bathroom. She comes as fast as she reasonably can. The cascade has started. You are managing two crying toddlers by the time she arrives. You are fine. You were built for this. It's just louder than it needed to be.

## D:win

Cocomelon comes on. The crying child stops. Not gradually. Immediately. Like a switch. She sits down on the carpet and stares at the screen with the focus of a child who has found her people. The room follows. For the remaining forty minutes of second service you have the quietest toddler room of the year. You will think about this on Mondays. You will not act on it. You will think about it.

## D:lose

Cocomelon comes on. The room reacts. The crying child stops. Six other toddlers gather. One parent, dropping off late, sees the TV. The parent knows you. The parent is also on the church's children's ministry advisory board. She makes a note. You will receive a very thoughtful email Monday morning with the subject line 'Screen Time Policy — Quick Thought.'
