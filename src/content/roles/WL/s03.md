Sound check starts at 8:30. You have 30 minutes and 90 minutes of things to do. Including the latest emergency.

The confidence monitor – the screen you rely on to show you the lyrics of the songs you're too lazy to memorize – is stuck on a glamour shot of the pastor's wife. Cody, your 17-year-old tech volunteer recruited from student ministry 3 weeks ago, is shrugging in a way that communicates he has done everything he knows how to do.

## A

You fix it. You're now 15 minutes behind on everything else. But Cody learns something and thinks you're cool.

## B

Fixed in 4 minutes. Tech leader is slightly annoyed to be bothered on vacation but handles it. Cody feels slightly humiliated.

## C

You sit backstage alone with headphones in, desperately trying to cram the lyrics into your head. You even jot a few notes on your wrist in Sharpie. It's still not enough.

## D:win

The band loses it. Jordan looks up from her phone for the first time all morning. Even the drummer — who just arrived and has no context — starts laughing. For approximately 90 seconds the entire worship team is unified in a way that no team cookout has ever achieved. Cody fixes the screen at 9:47 AM. Nobody outside the band ever sees it. The mustache becomes legend. Your keys player references it in their farewell speech two years later when they take a job at another church.

## D:lose

The band loses it. Jordan looks up from her phone for the first time all morning. The campus pastor does a pre-service walkthrough at 9:15 AM. He sees the screen. He sees his wife. He sees the mustache. He turns to look at you very slowly, in the way of a man who is deciding in real time how to feel about something. Cody is not in the room. You are holding a dry erase marker. The evidence is circumstantial but overwhelming.
