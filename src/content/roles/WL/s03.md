Sound check starts at 8:30. You have 30 minutes and 90 minutes of things to do. DeShawn is loading ProPresenter and has already flagged that two songs are missing the last verse. Kayla has just informed you, like a doctor telling you that you have an inoperable hemorrhoid, that she updated the streaming laptop's OS this morning and now it's not working.

The confidence monitor — the screen that faces the stage so you can see lyrics — is displaying a glamour shot of the pastor's wife. Cody, your 17-year-old tech volunteer recruited from student ministry 3 weeks ago, is shrugging in a way that communicates he has done everything he knows how to do.

## A

You fix it. You're now 15 minutes behind on everything else. But Cody learns something and thinks you're cool.

## B

Fixed in 4 minutes. Tech leader is slightly annoyed but handles it. Cody feels slightly humiliated.

## C

You do know these songs. Mostly. There's a bridge you always blank on. You file this away and tell yourself it will be fine.

## D:win

The band loses it. Jordan looks up from her phone for the first time all morning. Even the drummer — who just arrived and has no context — starts laughing. For approximately 90 seconds the entire worship team is unified in a way that no devotional has ever achieved. Cody fixes the screen at 9:47 AM. Nobody outside the band ever sees it. The mustache becomes legend. Your keys player references it in their farewell speech two years later when they take a job at another church.

## D:lose

The band loses it. Jordan looks up from her phone for the first time all morning. The campus pastor does a pre-service walkthrough at 9:15 AM. He sees the screen. He sees his wife. He sees the mustache. He turns to look at you very slowly, in the way of a man who is deciding in real time how to feel about something. Cody is not in the room. You are holding a dry erase marker. The evidence is circumstantial but overwhelming.
