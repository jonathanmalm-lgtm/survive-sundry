Call time was 7:30. Only 4 of your 7 band members have arrived. Classic. The drummer texted 'omw' at 7:28. He is always late. But you've made peace with it because he's the only drummer on your team who doesn't stress you out the whole set.

Your acoustic guitarist Jordan is here but sitting in the corner staring at her phone with the posture of someone who had a very bad Friday night. Your keys player is setting up and accidentally playing something that is not on the set list. Nobody comments on this.

Then she walks in. Patricia. Sixty-seven years old. Carrying an oboe case. Your set list is five Elevation Worship songs. Your brain is struggling to place an oboe part in the middle of the five different electric parts that will be playing. But Patricia has unpacked her oboe and is tuning it now.

You text the campus pastor: 'Hey — who is Patricia?' He responds in four minutes: 'Oh great she made it! She goes way back with the church. Just give her a spot 🙂' You text back: 'What would you like her to play?' Three minutes pass. 'Whatever works! You're the expert 😊'

Two emojis. This is escalating.

## A

You restructure the set. You find a quiet bridge where Patricia can play an eight-bar melodic line. Patricia agrees to play only that section. The eight bars are genuinely beautiful. Three people cry. None of them know why. On your way to the stage you check in with Jordan — she looks like she needs a moment and you give her one. Ron says the oboe was 'an interesting choice.' This is the most positive thing Ron will say all year.

## B

You learn: Patricia is widowed, has been coming 18 months, once mentioned she used to play in church orchestras. He said 'you should play with us sometime.' She took this seriously. He did not realize she took this seriously. 'What do you want me to do?' you ask. 'Whatever you think is right. I trust you.' He hangs up. You have the authority and the responsibility simultaneously. This is leadership.

## C

Patricia plays her heart out for all five songs. She is fully committed. She is feeling it. From the stage she looks like a woman who has finally found her moment. From the sound booth, her channel has been at zero the entire morning. The congregation hears nothing. Patricia hears everything. After service she finds you. 'I felt so free up there,' she says. You say: 'That's so great, Patricia.' You mean zero percent of this sentence and one hundred percent of this sentence simultaneously.

## D:win

You find a closet. You put the oboe in the closet. You compose your expression. Patricia returns. You deliver the news with the gravity of a man reporting a genuine tragedy. You talk Patricia out of calling the police. You retrieve the oboe after service and tell her it was 'miraculously recovered near the back entrance.' Patricia is overwhelmed with gratitude. She tells the campus pastor that God protected her oboe. The campus pastor tells the congregation this story the following week as an example of God's provision. You are present for this. You are standing on the stage. You do not make eye contact with anyone.

## D:lose

You find a closet. You put the oboe in the closet. You compose your expression. Patricia returns. You deliver the news with the gravity of a man reporting a genuine tragedy. Patricia is upset. Patricia is very upset. Patricia would like to call the police. The tech leader, who saw you put the oboe in the closet, mentions it to the campus pastor at the Hits and Misses meeting with the tone of someone who is not sure what they witnessed but feels it should be on record. The campus pastor looks at you. You look at the campus pastor. Patricia is in the lobby telling someone about the homeless man. The campus pastor's expression is a complete sentence. It is not a good sentence.
