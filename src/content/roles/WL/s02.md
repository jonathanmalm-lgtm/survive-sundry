Call time was 7:30. Only 4 of your 7 band members have arrived. Classic. The drummer texted 'omw' at 7:28. He is always late. But you've made peace with it because he's the only drummer on your team who doesn't stress you out the whole set.

Your acoustic guitarist, Jordan, is here, but she's sitting in the corner staring at her phone looking like she just figured out why her favorite band Nirvana hasn't released any new music.

Your keys player is noodling on the melody to a song that definitely doesn't belong in church.

That's when she walks in. Patricia. Fifty-seven years old. Carrying an oboe case. 'Hi {first name}! I'm Patricia. Pastor said I could sit in this morning,' she says.

Your dumbfounded. This morning's setlist is three Elevation songs. There are no oboe parts in Elevation songs. And you're struggling to imagine where a solo instrument like an oboe would fit into the mix of the five electric guitar parts that are programmed into the tracks.

But Patricia has unpacked her oboe and is tuning it now.

You text the campus pastor: 'Hey — who is Patricia?' He responds in two minutes: 'Oh great she made it! She goes way back with the church. Just give her a spot 🙂' You text back: 'What would you like her to play?' Three minutes pass. 'Whatever works! You're the expert 😊'

Two emojis. He's serious.

## A

You restructure the set and modify arrangements. You find a quiet bridge where Patricia can play an eight-bar melodic line. Patricia agrees to play only that section. The eight bars are genuinely beautiful. Three people cry. The good kind. It worked out this morning.

## B

You learn: Patricia is recently widowed, has been tuning into the livestream for 18 months with her husband, and mentioned she used to play in church orchestras. He said 'you should play with us sometime.' She took this seriously. He did not realize she took this seriously. 'What do you want me to do?' you ask. 'Whatever you think is right. I trust you.' He hangs up. You have the authority and the responsibility simultaneously. This is leadership. You decide to let her play and talk her through the process of joining the team after service.

## C

Patricia plays her heart out for all three songs. She is fully committed. She is feeling it. From the stage she looks like a woman who has finally found her moment. From the sound booth, her channel has been at zero the entire morning. You see people squinting during worship, trying to figure out why that lady is on stage apparently smoking a hookah.

You lose respect with at least four people who know exactly what went down (though they'll never tell you).

## D:win

You find a closet. You put the oboe in the closet. You prepare your expression. Patricia returns. You deliver the news with the gravity of a man reporting a genuine tragedy. You talk Patricia out of calling the police. You retrieve the oboe after service and tell her it was 'miraculously recovered near the back entrance.' Patricia is overwhelmed with gratitude. She tells the campus pastor that God protected her oboe. The campus pastor tells the congregation this story the following week as an example of God's provision. You aren't proud, but at least you didn't have to have her on stage the previous Sunday.

## D:lose

You find a closet. You put the oboe in the closet. You prepare your expression. Patricia returns. You deliver the news with the gravity of a man reporting a genuine tragedy. Patricia is very upset. Patricia calls the police.

The police come after service. You persist in the lie about the homeless man. Your keyboard player – who saw the whole thing – tells the police the whole truth and now you're outted as a cowardly liar to every one of your coworkers. Some think it's hilarious... Some don't.
