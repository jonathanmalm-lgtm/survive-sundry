The lobby is being set up. You've shot six things this morning that are genuinely good. The setup has that specific quality of a thing about to happen — gear being placed, conversations being started, volunteers finding their spots.

You could start posting now. Pre-service content builds engagement. You know this. You've made the case for it in meetings. The case was accepted. You now have the green light and the content and the time.

You also know that the campus pastor's feedback last week — delivered via the specific emoji that is not 😊 but is 🙂 — was about the pre-service content feeling 'behind the scenes in a way that doesn't represent us.' You are not entirely sure what that means. You are pretty sure it means the candid shots of the staff looking stressed should not be the first thing people see.

## A

You post three frames. They're good. Two of them land. One of them — the one where the worship leader is squinting at a monitor with his iced coffee — gets 47 likes in twenty minutes and two comments that say 'this is so real.' The campus pastor sees it. His response is: '🙂' You feel the space between the smile and the full smile.

## B

You hold the behind-the-scenes frames. You post a clean lobby shot with a caption at 9:55 AM — five minutes before service. The timing is right. The photo is right. The campus pastor's response is: '🙂😊' Two emojis. The 😊 is new. You file this.

## C

You post nothing until service starts. The feed goes from last Sunday to this Sunday without any pre-service content. You miss the engagement window. The first post of the morning is a worship shot during the second song. It performs fine. It performs the way your content always performs when you post at the second song. You've been trying to get off the second song for six months.

## D:win

Your personal story gets 340 views. Eighty-three of them are people who also attend the church. Three of them text you about the content. The campus pastor is not one of those eighty-three. The campus pastor does not follow your personal story. You post the safe version to the church account. Both things coexist. You feel the gap between them.

## D:lose

The campus pastor does follow your personal story. You didn't know this. He's never engaged with it. He sees the behind-the-scenes frame. He texts you at 9:34 AM: 'Is this on the church page?' 'Personal story,' you say. 'Okay,' he says. The 'okay' has the specific weight of a word that means 'we'll talk about this.' You spend the next forty minutes thinking about the okay.
