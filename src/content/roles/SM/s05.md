First service is starting. The lights are doing the thing. The band opens and the room opens with it — not all at once, but in the specific way rooms open when the first note lands right and the air changes.

You are in the back with your camera and you have about forty seconds before the moment is gone. The angle from the back is fine. The angle from the middle aisle is better. The angle from the front is the one that makes the shot — worship leader facing the congregation, band behind, faces in the crowd visible, the whole room in frame.

The angle from the front requires you to walk down the center aisle during the first thirty seconds of first service.

## A

You walk down the aisle. You are focused and deliberate. You get the shot in ninety seconds and return to the back. Two people watch you walk. One of them is Greg. Greg looks at you and then at the stage. His expression is analytical. The shot is the best photo you've taken in six months. The campus pastor's response is: '😊😊'

## B

You put the camera in your bag. You stand in the back and experience the first two songs. You feel the room in a way you haven't felt it in a while — you've been behind a camera long enough that sometimes you forget what the front of the glass looks like from the other side. You pick the camera back up for the third song. The shots from the third song are technically fine. Something in you is more present for the rest of the service.

## C

The back-of-room shots are safe. Safe content. You have a library of safe content. The safe content performs at a consistent level that nobody is excited about and nobody complains about and which will continue performing at a consistent level until someone asks why the page feels 'corporate.'

## D:win

You're at the front for thirty-five seconds. The worship leader sees you, keeps going — he's been shot mid-song before, he doesn't break focus. The shot is extraordinary. The congregation in the frame is fully present. It looks like something is happening because something is. The campus pastor calls it 'the best worship photo we've ever had.' You will hear this sentence again in three different meetings.

## D:lose

You're at the front. The worship leader sees you, keeps going. Twelve people in the first three rows see you move in front of them. Two of them lose the moment — you can see it in their faces. You get the shot. It's good. It's not good enough to justify what you see in two faces in frame. You crop them out. The campus pastor doesn't know. You know.
