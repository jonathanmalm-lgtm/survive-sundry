You're the first one in the building. You have a key. You go straight to the booth. You power everything up in the exact sequence you always do — the sequence you laminated and taped to the inside of the booth door after the incident last February that you do not talk about.

Everything comes up clean. Except the subwoofer. It makes a sound like a disappointed sigh and then goes quiet. Not broken-quiet. Suspicious-quiet. You put your hand on it. You can feel a faint hum.

## A

40 minutes. The amplifier card is failing. You know exactly how bad it is. You can plan around it — but you've lost 40 minutes of everything else.

## B

Professional. Creates a paper trail. Vendor doesn't respond until Monday. But when the pastor asks what happened, you have receipts.

## C

Bold. Clean. The worship leader will notice immediately. The bass frequencies will be 'present but polite.' Ron in row 4 will actually prefer it.

## D:win

There is no win. You step back from the subwoofer. You look at it. It hums with the new energy of something that has been asked to do more than it should. You nod at it once like you have reached an agreement. You have not reached an agreement.

## D:lose

The subwoofer survives sound check. It survives the first two songs. Halfway through the third it makes a sound that has no technical name but every audio engineer would recognize immediately. The bass frequency that exits in its final moment is so concentrated and so sudden that a kid running laps in the front row gets hit by the air displacement and goes down. Flat. On the carpet. He was not tripped. He was not pushed. He was knocked over by a sound wave from a subwoofer you turned up this morning because you had a feeling. He will tell this story for the rest of his life and nobody will fully believe him. His mom is already walking toward the booth. Ron is already composing his feedback. You are on your headset. It is 10:31 AM on a Sunday.
