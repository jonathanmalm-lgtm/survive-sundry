Third worship song. The tech leader's headset crackles: 'Livestream is down. I need two minutes.' The worship leader glances at you from the stage. The congregation doesn't know yet — they're mid-song. Greg is in the fourth row and he just checked his phone, which means he probably noticed the stream icon go gray.

The tech leader's voice again: 'This is a hardware issue. I can't bring it back before the message.'

This is the moment. You only preach when the livestream goes down. This is what you tell people as a joke. It is no longer a joke. The sermon you've been working on for four days is in a folder on your phone and also, more importantly, in your head. Thirty seconds from now you are going to walk into an auditorium and preach it to a live congregation while the tech leader tries to restore the feed.

## A

You walk out. The worship leader sees you coming and does an extra verse to give you a moment to settle. You take the mic. You preach. It is not the most polished sermon of your year. It is present in a way that polished sometimes isn't. Greg comes to you after service. 'You were ready,' he says. 'I'm always supposed to be,' you say. Greg nods.

## B

The tech team takes two minutes. The feed is not restored. You walk out. You preach. The extra two minutes of pressure have sharpened something. The sermon lands harder than the reviewed version would have. The tech leader restores the feed during the offering. Three people ask if the sermon is available online. It is not. That's okay.

## C

Three minutes. Five minutes. The tech leader texts: 'Working on it.' You are in the lobby. People inside can see the screen is blank behind the worship leader who is vamping through an extended version of the bridge. The worship leader has now done the bridge four times. The congregation is kind but beginning to wonder. Seven minutes in, you walk out. Your entrance at minute seven is less confident than it would have been at minute zero.

## D:win

The sermon you've been carrying in your head for three weeks — the better one, the one you didn't preach because the calendar said this week was the series topic — comes out instead. It is not polished. It is honest. Two people come to you afterward and say it was the best sermon they've heard this year. You do not tell them it was unplanned. You also do not tell them it was supposed to happen in three weeks. You go home and write it properly.

## D:lose

The sermon in your head is not the sermon in your notes. The sermon in your notes has the specific illustrations that go with the specific Scripture passage you announced in the bulletin. The sermon in your head is about something adjacent. You preach the thing in your head. Halfway through you realize you have not addressed the passage in the bulletin. You address it briefly at the end. Three people come to you afterward with questions about the passage you summarized. You answer them. You also rewrite next week's sermon on Monday.
